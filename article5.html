Japan's Economy on the Big Screen: From ‘Shoplifters’ to Reality

← Back to Articles
October 03, 2025 • Written by Hans • Ciomas, Indonesia • 7 min read

Japanese cinema often provides a raw, unfiltered look at the economic struggles hidden beneath the surface of a prosperous society.

The Two Faces of Japan

When viewed from the outside, Japan is often seen through a lens of economic marvel—a nation of high-speed trains, gleaming skyscrapers, and technological innovation. However, a closer look, particularly through the poignant narratives of its contemporary cinema, reveals a different, more complex story. Films like Hirokazu Kore-eda’s Palme d'Or-winning masterpiece, *Shoplifters* (2018), serve as powerful social commentaries, pulling back the curtain on a side of Japan often invisible to the world: the quiet struggles of its lower class and the cracks in its social safety net.

'Shoplifters': A Microcosm of Economic Precarity

*Shoplifters* tells the story of a makeshift family living on the fringes of Tokyo society. They rely on a grandmother's meager pension, low-wage precarious jobs, and petty theft to survive. The film doesn't sensationalize their poverty; instead, it presents it with a quiet, observational realism. This narrative is not fiction in the purest sense; it is a reflection of a growing reality in Japan. The characters represent the "working poor"—individuals who hold jobs but remain trapped in a cycle of poverty due to stagnant wages, the rise of non-regular employment ('freeters'), and a social welfare system that can be difficult to navigate.

From the Silver Screen to Economic Data

The story of the Shibata family in *Shoplifters* resonates deeply with Japan's macroeconomic trends over the past three decades. The "Lost Decades" were characterized by economic stagnation and deflation, which eroded wage growth and job security. While Japan maintains a low official unemployment rate, a significant portion of its workforce—nearly 40%—is employed in non-regular positions. These jobs often lack the benefits, stability, and opportunities for advancement that defined Japan's post-war corporate culture. This creates a dual-class labor market, where a growing segment of the population lives with economic insecurity despite being employed.

An Accountant's View: More Than Just a Story

From a financial and accounting perspective, the issues presented in films like *Shoplifters* are more than just compelling human drama; they are symptoms of underlying economic challenges. Stagnant wages directly impact consumer spending, a key driver of GDP. A large population of low-income workers puts a long-term strain on public finances, as tax revenues shrink while demand for social services grows. Furthermore, this level of income inequality can hinder social mobility and suppress the nation's overall potential for sustainable economic growth. These films remind us that a nation's economic health cannot be measured solely by its corporate profits or technological prowess, but must also account for the well-being and stability of its entire population.
